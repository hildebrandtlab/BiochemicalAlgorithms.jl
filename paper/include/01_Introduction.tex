\section{Introduction}


The aim of structure-based bioinformatics is the analysis and targeted manipulation of three-dimensional structures of biological macromolecules, such as, proteins and nucleic acids. This research field integrates diverse disciplines ranging from fundamental physical laws to complex biochemistry knowledge and advanced numerical computing methodologies. For example, in molecular mechanics a molecular force field can be used for the computation of the energy of a structure.  \\
Structure-based bioinformatics encompass various applications such as molecular modelling, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations and molecular docking scenarios.  Molecular modeling techniques and in particular docking suites were attracting widespread interest during the COVID-19 pandemic: The structure of virus proteins were predicted and examined including analysis of the effects of mutations originating from various virus strains. The knowledge of these molecular functions was then used in the context of rational drug design for the purpose of finding potential vaccine or drug therapeutics\cite{Kumar2021}. \\
The COVID-19 pandemic higlighted the importance of these applications. However, most open-source software packages for handling molecular structures and molecular applications were created between 1995 to 2010 \todo{cite something}. There are several reasons for the decreasing interest in this field. A crucial aspect is the availability of molecular structure data. Historically, the number of experimentally resolved structures was limited for many years leading to a slowdown of the progress in this area. In recent years, the number of experimentally structures with high resolution is increasing dramatically through advances in electron microscopy. With the rapid rise of computed structures, the availability no longer restrain the development of structural bioinformatics applications. \todo{find citation}\\
Software development in this field was, and still is, typically challenging due its interdisciplinary nature. The need for both numerical stability and computational efficiency, along with ease of use, has been a significant obstacle for rapid application development (RAD). 
It is, therefore, not uncommon, that many open-source software packages focused on the implementation of one specific task or algorithm e.g., the introduction of a docking algorithm leaving the responsibility for a properly preprocessed structure to the user \todo{cite single approaches} \\
An exception is the introduction of \ballFull (\ball) by Kohlbacher et al. in 1996.  \ball is a well-designed framework for molecular structure analysis, offering functionality such as file import and export, structure preprocessing, molecular mechanics, advanced solvation methods, and visualization options. \ball used to have one of the biggest user communities in this field as indicated by the long lasting maintenance and development. \\
Likewise, Schroedinger provide a platform with software packages for different molecular applications: \texttt{ProteinPreparationWizard} deals with the preprocessing of protein structural models including missing atoms, missing hydrogens, bond order assignment etc. or \texttt{LiveDesign} for the docking and design of ligands. Although Schroedinger provides entire platforms for several task, these have the indisputable disadvantage of being closed-source and not free of charge. \\
Another open-source software packages for molecular dynamics as been published in 2016. Similar to \ball it is also written in C++ with additional Python bindings \cite{Doerr2016HTMD}. While C++ is necessary for the required efficiency of the programms, it effectively hinders the rapid prototyping of molecular algorithms.  \\
Developing software for structural bioinformatics is still challenging, but choosing the programming language for it is not. Julia offers both the efficiency and numerical stability needed for molecular simulations along with the ability of rapid development. \\

Several packages already exist in Julia related to structural bioinformatics. Most prominently, the packages under the two Github communities \textit{Molecular Simulation in Julia} and \textit{BioJulia} \cite{JuliaMolSim, BioJulia}. The latter offers software packages for general biology approaches such as \textit{BioSymbols.jl} for the representation of nucleic and amino acid primitive types as well as packages related to sequential bioinformatics. \\

\textit{Molly.jl} is an excellent package for molecular simulations written in Julia and is part of the \textit{Molecular Simulation in Julia} Github community \cite{Greener2024}. \\
An interesting approach for handling oligopeptides is \textit{ProtoSyn.jl}. Although it provides functionalities for the analysis of peptides, there remains a need for general platform. More specifically, a platform from which molecular file formats can be read and write, the entire preprocessing pipeline can be integrated and the infrastructure for molecular mechanics are provided is still lacking. \\

We present \biochem as a general purpose framework for structure-based bioinformatics. \biochem provides the foundation for molecular modelling and molecular simulation studies encompassing the entire molecular modeling pipeline:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Reading common data formats such as PDB, hin, mol and JSON
	\item Preprocessing the input by preparing the entire system
	\item Molecular Mechanics such as AMBER ForceField
	\item Visualization of structures
\end{itemize}
In addition, the intuitive interfaces of \biochem enables users to develop own applications like own implementation of force fields. \\
